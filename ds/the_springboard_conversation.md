# The springboard conversation
This comes from Polish director Krzysztof Kieslowski in his book 'Kieslowski on Kieslowski'. He describes it as his 'springboard method' and as two different variants.  
The first variant goes likes this:  
- First is the 'outline', about a page. The whole thing first and the shortest of the whole, a page or less. No individual scenes, no individual solutions or individual characters. Not to play around with details or breaking the thing up into little pieces.  
- Then the 'novella' or 'treatment', about 20 pages, no or very little dialogue.  
- Then the script/shooting script.  

The second variant:  
- He writes a first version, not the one page outline. Instead he writes a sort of intuitive rendering of what the films going to be about so that the producer knows what the proposal involves. This outline does not cover the scale of the production becuase that not known at this stage yet. Usually just the idea thats put forward here. _(Comment: On this level, it can be discussed. We do not need to write documents, code, mockups, we can just discuss. What do we need here etc.)_  
Usually there is more than one version written of the outline.  
- Then he writes the treatment so that the producer can see the scale of the production. This is exceptionally important for Kieslowski becuase the treatment contains action or seeds of action and a sketch of the characters. There is no dialogue yet or only scraps of it. _(Kieslowski makes a brief comment, that 'it is sometimes descriptive' and this is important, discussing what and how, not write the final script even in a brief or rough version)_. But, at any rate the treatment is another version of the whole.  
- Then the script is written. About 150-200 pages or one page/minute. Shooting scripts is not done because it is not needed and Kieslowski says he dont get paid for it anyway.
- When filming, all crew and actors comes in and everything changes...  
- Usually also there are more than one version written of both the treatment and script.
